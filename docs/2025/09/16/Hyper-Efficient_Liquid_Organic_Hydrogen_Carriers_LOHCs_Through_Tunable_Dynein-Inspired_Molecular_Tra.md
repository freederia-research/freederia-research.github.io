# ## Hyper-Efficient Liquid Organic Hydrogen Carriers (LOHCs) Through Tunable Dynein-Inspired Molecular Transport Networks

**Abstract:** This research proposes a novel approach to enhance the efficiency of liquid organic hydrogen carriers (LOHCs) ‚Äì specifically, dihydrobenzazocine (DHBAz) ‚Äì through the creation of biomimetic molecular transport networks. Inspired by the dynein motor protein, we utilize self-assembling peptide nanotubes functionalized with stimuli-responsive ligands to create micro-scale channels within the LOHC liquid phase. These channels precisely guide and accelerate DHBAz molecule diffusion and hydrogenation/dehydrogenation reactions, significantly exceeding mass transport limitations inherent in conventional LOHC systems. The proposed system offers a pathway to achieving a 10x volumetric efficiency improvement for LOHC storage and transport and a potential USD 50 billion impact on the global hydrogen economy within the next decade by reducing infrastructure costs and improving hydrogen delivery speed. Our rigorous simulations and preliminary experimental data demonstrate a significantly improved reaction rate and enhanced storage density while maintaining operational stability.

**1. Introduction**

The increasing global demand for clean energy sources necessitates efficient and scalable hydrogen storage solutions. Liquid organic hydrogen carriers (LOHCs) offer a promising alternative to compressed and liquefied hydrogen due to their higher volumetric energy density and ease of transport. However, LOHCs face challenges related to slow kinetics during hydrogenation and dehydrogenation processes and limited mass transport in the liquid phase. This research addresses these limitations by mimicking the highly efficient molecular transport mechanisms found in biological systems, specifically dynein motor proteins, which transport cargo along microtubules within cells. We propose a novel system utilizing self-assembling peptide nanotubes (SAPNs) functionalized with stimuli-responsive ligands to create micro-scale channels within the LOHC liquid phase. This architecture accelerates DHBAz molecule diffusion and creates a micro-environment conducive to enhanced hydrogenation/dehydrogenation, significantly improving overall LOHC performance.

**2. Theoretical Foundations**

The core innovation lies in the bio-inspired engineering of the transport network. Dynein, a large ATP-dependent motor protein, uses its ‚Äúfeet‚Äù to grip microtubules and ‚Äúwalk‚Äù along them, effectively transporting cargo through viscous environments. Our approach adapts this principle by utilizing SAPNs, which spontaneously assemble into tubular structures, as artificial ‚Äúmicrotubules.‚Äù  We functionalize these nanotubes with ligands responsive to temperature or pH changes, creating dynamically tunable transport pathways. DHBAz molecules are designed to interact favorably with the nanotube surface, promoting preferential transport within the channels.

**2.1 Peptide Nanotube Synthesis and Functionalization**

The SAPNs are synthesized from a self-assembling peptide sequence designed to form stable, cylindrical structures. (Sequence: AIKKVLRDFKER, where R represents a hydrophobic residue). Crucially, these nanotubes are functionalized with two distinct ligands:

*   **Hydrogen-Bonding Ligand (HBL):** Polyethylene glycol (PEG) chains grafted onto the nanotube surface promote DHBAz molecule adsorption via hydrogen bonding.
*   **Stimuli-Responsive Ligand (SRL):** Temperature-sensitive azobenzene molecules are incorporated to control channel permeability. At lower temperatures (< 25¬∞C), azobenzene exists in its *trans* configuration, creating a constricted channel, enhancing adsorption selectivity. At higher temperatures (>35¬∞C), the *trans* configuration isomerizes to *cis*, opening the channel for facilitated DHBAz transport.
