The **Genetic Algorithm (GA)** operates like this:

1.  **Initialization:**  Start with a population of "individuals," each representing a unique combination of ligands on the QDs.
2.  **Fitness Evaluation:** Use the DFT model to predict the conductivity of each individual. The "fitness" is how close the predicted conductivity is to the desired target value (the closer, the fitter).
3.  **Selection:** Select the ‚Äúfittest‚Äù individuals to become ‚Äúparents‚Äù of the next generation.  "Binary tournament selection" means randomly selecting two individuals and picking the one with the better fitness.
4.  **Crossover:**  Combine genetic material (ligand configurations) from the parents to create "offspring." "Single-point crossover" takes two parent ligand combinations and swaps genetic information at a single point.
5.  **Mutation:** Randomly alter some of the genes (ligand types or concentrations) in the offspring.  "Gaussian mutation" adds a small, random value, simulating a slight change in the ligand environment.
6.  **Repeat:**  Go back to step 2 with the new population, iteratively improving the ligand configurations over many "generations."

The control parameters (Œº=30, Œª=50, Pc=0.8, Pm=0.05) define these specific settings for population size, mutation rate, and crossover probability, further influencing the algorithm's performance.
