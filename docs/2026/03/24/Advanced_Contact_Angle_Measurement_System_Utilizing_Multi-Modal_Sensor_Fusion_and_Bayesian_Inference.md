# ## Advanced Contact Angle Measurement System Utilizing Multi-Modal Sensor Fusion and Bayesian Inference for Enhanced Material Characterization

**Abstract:** This paper presents a novel contact angle measurement system leveraging multi-modal sensor fusion and Bayesian inference to achieve significantly improved accuracy and robustness in material characterization compared to conventional methods. The system integrates high-resolution optical microscopy, advanced shear force sensing, and environmental control, enabling dynamic assessment of surface wettability across a wide range of conditions. A Bayesian inference framework effectively synthesizes data from these modalities, mitigating limitations associated with each individual sensor and providing a comprehensive and reliable assessment of surface properties. This approach surpasses current limitations in measuring dynamic contact angles on complex, non-uniform surfaces, offering significant advancements for industries including microfluidics, coatings, and biomedical device manufacturing.

**1. Introduction**

Contact angle measurement is a fundamental technique for characterizing surface wettability – a crucial property impacting diverse applications ranging from microfluidic device design to the performance of protective coatings. Traditional methods, relying primarily on optical imaging, face limitations when dealing with complex surfaces exhibiting non-uniformity, dynamic behavior, or challenging lighting conditions.  These shortcomings often lead to inconsistencies and inaccurate representations of the true surface wettability.  This research addresses these limitations by introducing an advanced contact angle measurement system which integrates multiple sensing modalities and employs Bayesian inference to create a robust and accurate assessment of surface properties. By fusing data from optical microscopy, shear force sensing, and precise environmental control, we overcome individual sensor limitations, achieving a more comprehensive and reliable characterization of dynamic and complex surfaces.

**2. System Design & Methodology**

The proposed system comprises three primary integrated components: (1) a high-resolution optical microscopy module, (2) a sensitive shear force sensing module, and (3) a controlled environment chamber.  These are orchestrated by a Bayesian inference engine which dynamically weighs and fuses data streams to produce a robust final contact angle prediction. We’ve specifically targeted challenges related to droplet hysteresis and surface non-homogeneity, areas where conventional techniques often falter.

**2.1 Optical Microscopy Module:**  A custom-built optical system equipped with a long-working-distance objective lens (10x magnification, NA 0.5) captures high-resolution images of the droplet. Image processing algorithms, including edge detection (Canny edge detector), and droplet boundary fitting (least-squares ellipse fitting) are used to determine the initial droplet contour. This forms the foundation for the dynamic contact angle calculation.

**2.2 Shear Force Sensing Module:**  A miniature force-sensing cantilever system, calibrated for sensitivity in the nano-Newton range, is precisely positioned near the three-phase contact line (TPCL). This sensor measures subtle shear forces generated by the droplet's interaction with the surface, providing critical information about the surface heterogeneity and dynamic interfacial behavior, enabling effective reduction of hysteresis errors. The sensor utilizes a piezoresistive beam, demonstrating a sensitivity of 1 nN/µstrain. A data acquisition system samples shear force data at 1 kHz.

**2.3 Controlled Environment Chamber:** Temperature and humidity are meticulously controlled within the chamber using a Peltier-based system and a humidity sensor with an accuracy of ±1%. Precise environmental control allows  for analysis of materials under various conditions, revealing how wettability is affected by external stimuli.

**2.4 Bayesian Inference Engine:** The core innovation lies in the Bayesian inference engine. This engine utilizes a probabilistic model to incorporate data from all three modalities, quantifying the uncertainty associated with each measurement. The framework employs Bayes' theorem to update the prior belief about the contact angle based on the observed data from each sensor.

*Prior Distribution:* A Gaussian distribution is selected as the prior, reflecting the initial belief about the contact angle before considering sensor data.
*Likelihood Function:* The likelihood function describes the probability of observing the sensor data given a specific contact angle value. Foam-based models and moving least squares regression are adopted to calculate the likelihood functions and reduce variance.
*Posterior Distribution:* The posterior distribution represents the updated belief about the contact angle after considering the sensor data. The Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method provides a robust route for calculating the posterior distribution by traversing cultural spaces to identify maxima.

Mathematically, this is described as:

𝑃(𝜃|𝐷) ∝ 𝑃(𝐷|𝜃) 𝑃(𝜃)

Where:

*   *θ* represents the contact angle.
*   *D* represents the combined sensor data (optical images, shear force measurements, and environmental conditions).
*   *P(θ|D)* is the posterior distribution - probability of the contact angle given the data.
*   *P(D|θ)* is the likelihood function - probability of the data given a specific contact angle.
*   *P(θ)* is the prior distribution - initial belief about the contact angle.

**3. Experimental Design & Data Analysis**

To validate the system, we conducted experiments on a range of materials exhibiting varying surface properties: (1) Hydrophilic glass, (2) Hydrophobic Teflon, (3) Textured silicon wafers, (4) Bio-polymer thin films. A deionized water droplet (2 µL) was dispensed onto each surface, and the contact angle was measured in real-time.  Each material was tested under five temperature conditions (20°C, 25°C, 30°C, 35°C, and 40°C) and three humidity controls (30%, 50%, and 70% RH). For each condition, a minimum of 50 contact angle measurements were performed and averaged. The shear force data was analyzed for subtle variations related to surface heterogeneity.

**Data Analysis:** The measured contact angles were compared with those obtained from a conventional optical contact angle goniometer. Statistical analyses (t-tests, ANOVA) were conducted to evaluate the statistical significance of the improvements. Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) was used to quantify the difference between the two measurement methods.  The fractional bias (FB) was calculated to assess systematic errors. Further, gradient descent on the convergence speed and residuals helped identify optimal learning rates.

The system effectively captured dynamic contact angle behaviors on textured surfaces, which are poorly characterized by conventional methods. The shear force sensing module successfully identified and quantified surface heterogeneity, allowing the Bayesian inference engine to compensate for the impact of non-uniformity on the contact angle measurement.  The extended dynamic range demonstrates the system's ability to characterize strongly hydrophilic and hydrophobic surfaces.

# Commentary

## Commentary on Advanced Contact Angle Measurement System

This research addresses a critical challenge in materials science: accurately measuring how liquids interact with solid surfaces, known as contact angle. This interaction dictates the behavior of many products from coatings to microfluidic devices, yet traditional measurement techniques can be unreliable, particularly with complex or dynamic surfaces. The core innovation presented here is a system that combines multiple measurement tools and a powerful analytical technique—Bayesian inference—to overcome these limitations, offering significant improvements in accuracy and robustness.

**1. Research Topic & Core Technologies Explained**

Contact angle measurements quantify the angle formed where a liquid meets a solid. A low contact angle suggests the surface is “hydrophilic” (water-loving), while a high angle means the surface is “hydrophobic” (water-repelling). These properties are vital in diverse fields: microfluidics relies on precisely controlled wettability to manipulate fluids; coatings need the right wettability to adhere and perform effectively; and biomedical devices interact directly with biological fluids, making surface wetting crucial for biocompatibility. Traditional methods using optical imaging struggle with surfaces that are uneven, changing over time, or difficult to illuminate consistently. This leads to inconsistent and inaccurate data.

This new system combats this by weaving together three key technologies. **High-resolution optical microscopy** provides a detailed visual image of the droplet, allowing precise identification of its edge. **Shear force sensing** detects tiny forces between the droplet and the surface – forces that are often missed by optical methods but reflect subtle surface features. Finally, **environmental control** lets the researchers test materials under different temperatures and humidity—crucial because wettability is often temperature and humidity-dependent.

The real magic, however, lies in the **Bayesian inference engine**. Bayesian inference is a powerful statistical method, unlike conventional approaches which often rely on averaging data. Instead, Bayesian inference works by updating a "belief" (called a *prior distribution*) about the contact angle, integrating new information from each sensor.  Imagine you initially believe the contact angle is somewhere around 90 degrees. As the optical microscope gives you some data, and the shear force sensor provides complementary information, the Bayesian engine allows it to *refine* that belief, converging on a more accurate contact angle measurement. The prior, calculated likelihood functions, and posterior distribution create a mathematically rigorous method of handling complex, dynamic interactions.

**Why these technologies are important:** Optical microscopy visually illuminates the droplet, and shear force sensing captures dynamic surface data that could easily be missed by standard methods. Prior methods have latency and cannot handle dynamic data well. The integration of these three technologies creates a holistic data stream and produces more reliable results.


**Key Questions & Technical Advantages/Limitations:**

The primary technical advantage is the system's ability to handle complex, non-uniform surfaces and dynamic contact angles, an area where traditional goniometers often fail. The shear force sensor specifically addresses the problem of “droplet hysteresis”– the difference you observe in contact angle when you advance or retract the droplet. This hysteresis results from surface irregularities; the Bayesian inference engine effectively accounts for these irregularities.

A limitation, however, is the complexity of the system. Integrating three different sensors and a sophisticated inference engine requires specialized expertise and potentially adds to the system's cost. Furthermore, the accuracy of the Bayesian inference relies on the accuracy of the individual sensor measurements and the appropriate selection of prior distributions and likelihood functions.



**2. Mathematical Model & Algorithm Explained**

The heart of the system is the Bayesian inference engine. It uses a mathematical framework to combine data from the optical microscope, shear force sensor, and environmental controls.  The core equation is:

*𝑃(𝜃|𝐷) ∝ 𝑃(𝐷|𝜃) 𝑃(𝜃)*

Let’s break it down, using a simple, everyday analogy: Imagine you're trying to guess if it will rain tomorrow.

*   *𝜃 (Theta)*: This represents the 'contact angle' in the research, but think of it as ‘rain tomorrow.’
*   *𝐷 (D)*: This is the ‘data’ – what you observe. It could be the weather forecast, the cloud cover, the season, etc. In this research, *D* is the data from optical images, shear force measurements, and environmental conditions.
*   *𝑃(𝜃|𝐷)*: This is the **posterior probability** - your best guess for 'rain tomorrow' (*𝜃*) given all the data you've seen (*D*).
*   *𝑃(𝐷|𝜃)*: This is the **likelihood function** - how likely you are to see the data you’ve seen (*D*) if it does rain tomorrow (*𝜃*). For example, if it rains, you’d expect clouds and potentially wet streets.
*   *𝑃(𝜃)*: This is the **prior probability** – your initial belief about 'rain tomorrow' *before* looking at any data. Maybe historically, your location experiences rain 10% of the time, so your prior is 0.1.

The equation says: "The probability of rain tomorrow, given all the data, is proportional to how likely the data is if it rains, multiplied by your initial belief about rain."

To calculate this, the system uses practical tools. A Gaussian distribution represents the initial belief about the contact angle - a sort of assumption that the measurement is normally distributed around an expected value. Foam-based models and moving least squares regression are used to define the likelihood function, accounting for uncertainty in each sensor reading. Finally,  Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method is used to iteratively estimate the posterior from the GIaussian distribution. This uses a combination of Monte Carlo simulations and Markov processes. Each “chain” approximates the full posterior, resulting in a complete distribution.

**3. Experiment and Data Analysis Method**

To test the system, researchers used materials with a range of wetting properties: glass (hydrophilic), Teflon (hydrophobic), silicon wafers (textured – uneven surface), and biodegradable polymer films. They placed a tiny droplet (2 microliters) of deionized water onto each surface and measured its contact angle in real-time.

The experiment was designed to be very controlled. Each material was tested at five different temperatures (20-40°C) and three humidity levels (30%, 50%, and 70% relative humidity). For each condition, they obtained over 50 contact angle readings, then averaged them. Critically, they also recorded shear force data at a high rate (1000 times per second) to detect subtle surface variations.

Data analysis was then performed: comparing results with a traditional optical contact angle goniometer (the standard method). Statistical tests (t-tests, ANOVA) were used to see if the new system produced significantly better results.  **Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE)** calculated the overall difference between the two methods.  **Fractional Bias (FB)** indicated whether the new system tended to systematically over- or underestimate the contact angle, highlighting potential systematic errors.  Slope and convergence speed in gradient descent were also observed.



**Experimental Setup Description:**

The system's components were meticulously controlled. The Peltier-based system maintained accurate temperature regulation, while the humidity sensor with ±1% accuracy ensured precise humidity control. The 10x magnification objective offered high-resolution imaging and the cantilever system measures the forces generated by stress on the surface. The combination of tools allows the researchers to prevent systematic data error.


**Data Analysis Techniques:**

Regression analysis was used to model the relationship between variables like temperature, humidity, surface heterogeneity (as determined by shear force) and the calculated contact angle. Statistical analyses like ANOVA were used to test if the differences in data between the two measurement systems were statistically significant. For example, if the RMSE was 2.8 µ° for the new system and 7.2 µ° for the traditional one, ANOVA would determine whether the 4.4 µ° difference was simply random variation, or a real improvement.
